# Topic E — Recording Consent: Design Considerations (not legal advice)

## 1. Israel: the home-jurisdiction baseline

**Israel is one-party consent and remains so as of mid-2026.** The Secret Monitoring Law 5739-1979 (חוק האזנת סתר) defines prohibited "secret monitoring" as listening/recording **without the consent of any party** to the conversation — so a participant recording their own meeting is outside the offense ([RecordingLaw — Israel, reviewed May 2026](https://www.recordinglaw.com/world-recording-laws/israel-recording-laws/); Hebrew practitioner summaries updated Feb 2026: [exm.co.il](https://www.exm.co.il/features/call-recording/call-recording-laws.html), [hahn-law.co.il](https://www.hahn-law.co.il/secret-recording-wiretap-laws-admissibility-israel/)). Third-party interception is a crime (up to 3 years). Caveat from Israeli case law: a participant recording made **for the purpose of committing an offense or a tort** (e.g., extortion) loses the participant exemption — a niche but real edge.

**Proposed changes:** Two-party-consent proposals have surfaced repeatedly (Netanyahu floated it in [2016](https://www.timesofisrael.com/netanyahu-mulls-2-party-consent-for-phone-call-recordings/) and again per [Israel Hayom](https://www.israelhayom.co.il/news/article/13323045); a bill barring recording of public servants was [shelved](https://www.timesofisrael.com/netanyahu-orders-bill-barring-recording-of-public-servants-shelved/)). **None has passed.** UNCERTAIN: whether a 2025 revival is actively progressing — counsel should monitor; a switch to all-party consent in Israel would invalidate the product's silent-by-default posture.

**The real 2024-2026 change is Amendment 13 to the Protection of Privacy Law (passed Aug 2024, effective Aug 14, 2025)** ([IAPP](https://iapp.org/news/a/israel-marks-a-new-era-in-privacy-law-amendment-13-ushers-in-sweeping-reform); [ICLG Israel 2025-26](https://iclg.com/practice-areas/data-protection-laws-and-regulations/israel/)). It does not touch one-party consent, but it makes the *downstream processing* riskier: expanded "personal data"/"especially sensitive data" definitions, enhanced notice duties, PPA administrative fines (₪1,000–640,000+), statutory damages without proof of harm (up to ₪100,000 in some claims), DPO requirements for some entities, and a 7-year limitation period. A library of recorded VC meetings with speaker identities is plausibly a regulated **database**; transcripts of founders discussing finances/health/legal matters can hit "especially sensitive" categories. Even where *recording* is legal, *disclosure/use* of recordings can separately violate the Privacy Protection Law (up to 5 years for willful invasions).

**Workplace nuance:** Israel's PPA and the National Labor Court (CCTV decision, Mar 26, 2025 — [Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2025-05-06/israel-court-addresses-employees-right-to-workplace-privacy)) require legitimacy, proportionality, transparency, and advance notice for employee monitoring ([DataGuidance — Israel Employee Monitoring](https://www.dataguidance.com/notes/israel-employee-monitoring); [Lexology on Amendment 13 & employment](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=70cfbe1d-0f58-42fd-b52f-c57b4412be09)). Recording *internal* meetings (the 5 IN Venture users recording each other, HR conversations, comp discussions) is governed by this stricter employment-privacy lens, not just the Eavesdropping Law.

## 2. Counterparty jurisdictions

- **US:** ~11-12 all-party-consent states, including **California, Washington, Florida**, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Delaware ([Justia 50-state survey](https://www.justia.com/50-state-surveys/recording-phone-calls-and-conversations/)). California CIPA §632 carries **statutory damages of $5,000 per violation** and is the engine of the current AI-notetaker litigation wave. Practitioners' norm for interstate/international calls: **apply the strictest participant's rule**. An Israeli VC calling a Sand Hill Road GP or a Seattle founder is squarely in all-party territory; Israeli legality does not insulate against CIPA civil exposure for a recording made of a California participant (conflict-of-laws is genuinely unsettled — flag for counsel).
- **EU/GDPR:** Recording + diarized transcription of identifiable people is personal-data processing; the "household exemption" won't cover VC business meetings. Lawful basis is typically **legitimate interest with prior notice and right to object** rather than consent (consent is withdrawable and hard to evidence) ([IAPP on call recording bases](https://iapp.org/news/a/can-call-centers-rely-on-legitimate-interests-for-audio-recordings); [GDPR Local](https://gdprlocal.com/gdpr-recording-calls/)). Systematic recording likely warrants a **DPIA**; retention limits and deletion rights apply. Israel holds an EU adequacy decision — useful, but it raises the stakes of visible non-compliance.
- **UK:** Individuals recording calls they're party to for own use is lawful (RIPA 2000 framework); the trap is **sharing/processing** — passing recordings to Claude/Drive moves it into UK GDPR territory with notice/retention duties ([RecordingLaw UK](https://www.recordinglaw.com/world-laws/world-recording-laws/uk-recording-laws/)).

## 3. What no-bot competitors actually do

- **Granola** (closest architectural comparable): no recording indicator visible to other participants; ships an **optional "Automatic Consent Messaging"** feature that auto-pastes a notice into the meeting chat — currently **Zoom-on-macOS only** (Google Meet support paused) ([Granola docs](https://docs.granola.ai/help-center/consent-security-privacy/getting-consent)). Posture: "strongly recommends" asking consent, "you are responsible" disclaimers, audio deleted after transcription (transcript retained). Their enterprise guidance recommends two-stage disclosure (calendar-invite notice + verbal confirmation captured in transcript) and org-wide retention/auto-delete policies ([Granola blog](https://www.granola.ai/blog/ai-notetaker-participant-privacy-consent)). Notably, their guidance is ethics-framed and **silent on jurisdictional rules**.
- **Fathom** (bot-based): pre-meeting **consent email** to attendees; if an attendee declines, auto-record is disabled (host can override); the bot itself is visible in the call ([Fathom help](https://help.fathom.video/en/articles/294272)).
- **Otter** (bot-based): admin-enforceable **pre-meeting notification emails** plus the bot's join announcement ([Otter help](https://help.otter.ai/hc/en-us/articles/13353091821591-Enforce-pre-meeting-recording-notifications)) — and is the defendant in **In re Otter.AI Privacy Litigation (5:25-cv-06911, N.D. Cal.)**, four consolidated class actions (Aug-Sep 2025) alleging ECPA/CIPA violations for recording without all-party consent; consolidated complaint Dec 2025, MTD briefing through Apr 2026, ongoing ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/15/g-s1-83087/otter-ai-transcription-class-action-lawsuit); [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/ai-notetaking-tools-under-fire-lessons-otterai-class-action-complaint)).
- **Industry norm for local-capture tools:** silence by default + optional disclosure helper + responsibility-shifting ToS. There is **no norm of refusing to record**; the differentiation axis is how good the optional disclosure tooling is.

## 4. Platform/OS reality the design must account for

Local capture **bypasses every platform-native recording notice**: Zoom/Meet/Teams participants see nothing (that's the product's premise). On the user's own Mac, capturing system audio via Core Audio process taps triggers a **permission prompt (NSAudioCaptureUsageDescription) and a purple/orange privacy indicator in the menu bar/Control Center** ([Apple docs](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/CoreAudio/capturing-system-audio-with-core-audio-taps), [NSAudioCaptureUsageDescription](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/bundleresources/information-property-list/nsaudiocaptureusagedescription)) — visible only to the recordist, and visible to anyone watching a screen-share of the menu bar.

## 5. Design mitigations to surface (for counsel review)

1. **Per-meeting consent metadata field** in the context package: `consent_status` (verbal-obtained / calendar-notice / none / internal), jurisdiction hints from calendar attendee domains/timezones. Cheap, auditable, future-proof.
2. **Jurisdiction-aware nudge**: if calendar attendees resolve to US all-party states / EU domains, show a pre-record banner suggesting verbal disclosure; never block (norm: inform, don't enforce).
3. **Verbal-disclosure capture**: prompt a one-line script ("I use an AI notes tool — OK?") and mark the affirmation timestamp in the transcript — Granola's recommended best practice, contemporaneous evidence.
4. **Optional auto-chat-message** (Granola-style) per platform where feasible; hard on Meet-in-browser, easiest on Zoom.
5. **Calendar-invite boilerplate** snippet users can add to invites (asynchronous notice = the GDPR-friendly path).
6. **Retention controls**: configurable auto-delete of raw audio immediately post-transcription (Granola precedent), transcript retention windows, per-meeting "delete everything" action — directly responsive to Amendment 13 and GDPR minimization.
7. **Do-not-record defaults**: exclusion rules for internal/HR/1-on-1 calendar events (keyword/attendee-based), given Israeli employment-privacy exposure for internal recordings.
8. **Drive folder access control**: least-privilege sharing, no org-wide links; recordings library may constitute a PPL "database" — needs security measures and possibly a defined database policy.
9. **Decisions for counsel**: (a) is the meeting-recordings corpus a registrable/regulated database under Amendment 13, and is a DPO needed? (b) lawful basis + DPIA for EU founders; (c) CIPA exposure when recording CA/WA/FL counterparties from Israel and whether disclosure language mitigates; (d) internal-employee recording policy + written notice; (e) retention schedule; (f) what consent evidence to store and for how long.
